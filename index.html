Part 1

The History of Smooth Random Convex Hulls

Historical context behind geometric regularity in stochastic processes. We outline the decades-long pursuit to determine whether the boundaries of random paths (like Brownian motion and Lévy processes) are smooth or if some bits of their paths remain in sharp volumes like corners and thorns.

Smoothness Timeline

  • Paul Lévy (1948)

    Observed smoothness for planar Brownian motion.

  • El Bachir (1983)

    Analysed convex hulls of Brownian motion.

  • Cranston, Hsu & March (1989)

    Rigorously established smoothness of $\partial H$ for planar Brownian motion.

  • Alexander & Eldan (2019)

    Extended smoothness of Brownian motion to arbitrary dimension $d \geqslant 2$.

  • González Cázares, Kramer-Bang & Mijatović (2024)

    Characterised the class of 1D Lévy processes with smooth time-space convex hulls.

Singularities Timeline

  • Evans (1985) & Burdzy (1985)

    Studied Brownian cone points and thorns.

  • Burdzy & Kulczycki (2003)

    Proved isotropic stable processes have one-sided thorn points.

  • Alexander & Eldan (2019)

    Proved Brownian motion does not possess two-sided cone points in arbitrary dimensions.

The Open Question:

Does boundary smoothness exist beyond Brownian motion? What is the generic behaviour of multidimensional Lévy processes?

Boundary Parametrisation & Local Geometry

What is the subject of study and what does it look like?

The Closed Convex Hull in $\mathbb{R}^d$

Let $X = (X_t)_{t \geqslant 0}$ be a Lévy process in $\mathbb{R}^d$, $d \geqslant 2$, and fix $T \in (0, \infty]$. We study the closed convex hull of its path:

$$H := \mathrm{cl}\,(\text{Conv}\{X_t : t \in [0, T)\})$$

Supporting Hyperplanes & Normal Cones

Every $x \in \partial H$ has an inward-pointing normal cone:

$$\mathfrak{N}(x) := \{\theta \in \mathbb{R}^d_0 : H \subset x + \mathrm{cl}\,\mathfrak{L}_\theta\} \cup \{0\}$$

where $\mathfrak{L}_\theta := \{y \in \mathbb{R}^d : \langle \theta, y \rangle > 0\}$ denotes the positive half-space in the direction of $\theta\in\mathbb{R}^d_0:=\mathbb{R}^d\setminus\{0\}$. Similarly, each $\theta\in\mathbb{R}^d_0$ exposes a face:

$$\mathfrak{F}_\theta := \Big\{x \in H : \langle \theta, x \rangle = I_\theta:=\inf_{y\in H}\langle\theta,y\rangle\Big\}$$

Consider the last time the directional infimum (or exposed face) is approached:

$$\eta_\theta := \sup\{t \in [0,T) : \langle\theta, X_t\rangle \!\wedge\! \langle\theta, X_{t-}\rangle = I_\theta\}$$

We select a point in the exposed face by tracking the position of $X$ at this time:

$$\mathcal{I}_\theta := \begin{cases} X_{\eta_\theta}, & \text{if } \langle\theta, X_{\eta_\theta}\rangle = I_\theta, \\ X_{\eta_\theta-}, & \text{if } \langle\theta, X_{\eta_\theta-}\rangle = I_\theta. \end{cases}$$

Classifying Singularities

The geometry at $x \in \partial H$ is classified by the dimension of its normal cone, $\dim \mathfrak{N}(x)$:

  • Smooth Point
    $\dim \mathfrak{N}(x) = 1$. The normal cone is a single 1D ray.
  • Edge Point of Degree $r$
    $\dim \mathfrak{N}(x) = d - r$.
  • Corner
    $\dim \mathfrak{N}(x) = d$ (i.e., $r=0$). The most extreme singularity, where the normal cone constitutes a full $d$-dimensional volume.

Part 3

Main Global Results

Main theorems established in this project. The central breakthrough is identifying a broad class of processes $\mathfrak{D}$ whose convex hulls lack corners a.s., conditions for local smoothness a.s. and exploring the geometric limitations when analysing dimensions $d \geqslant 3$.

Theorem (Chaumont, GC, Mijatović, 2026+).

For any $X \in \mathfrak{D}$, $\partial H$ has no corners a.s. (In dimension $d=2$, $\partial H$ is smooth a.s.)

Corollary: This property is preserved under locally diffeomorphic transformations (full rank Jacobian).
Corollary: Lévy processes in $\mathfrak{D}$ a.s. do not possess two-sided corner points.

Defining the Regularity Classes $\mathfrak{D}$ and $\mathfrak{D}'$

Define the class $\mathfrak{D}$ of Lévy processes $X$ with: $$\sup_{\theta \in \mathbb{R}^d_0} \dim N_\theta < d,$$ where the deterministic cone $N_\theta$ is defined as the set of all $\theta'\in\mathbb{R}^d_0$ such that $$\int_0^1 \mathbb{P}(\langle \theta, X_t \rangle \langle \theta', X_t \rangle < 0) \frac{dt}{t}.$$ Define the class $\mathfrak{D}'\subset\mathfrak{D}$ of Lévy processes with $$\sup_{\theta \in \mathbb{R}^d_0} \dim M_\theta < d,$$ where the deterministic cone $M_\theta\supset N_\theta$ is defined as set of all $\theta'\in\mathbb{R}^d_0$ such that $$\liminf_{t\downarrow 0}\mathbb{P}(\langle \theta, X_t \rangle \langle \theta', X_t \rangle < 0)=0.$$

Prominent Members of $\mathfrak{D}'\subset\mathfrak{D}$

  • $\alpha$-stable processes with full support (for $\alpha \in (0,2)$).
  • Processes in the small-time domain of attraction of such an $\alpha$-stable process.
  • Isotropic processes of infinite activity.
  • Processes in $\mathfrak{D}'$ subordinated by an independent subordinator $X \circ \Gamma$.
  • Processes in $\mathfrak{D}'$ with arbitrary modifications to its Lévy measure outside a neighbourhood of the origin.
    ($\mathfrak{D}$ also has this invariance.)

Local Differentiability & Bounding the Normal Cone

Theorem (Rogozin for cones around extrema; Chaumont, GC, Mijatović, 2026+).
Fix $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^d_0$ with $I_\theta > -\infty$ a.s.

  1. If $N_\theta \neq \mathbb{R}^d$, then $\mathfrak{F}_\theta$ is a.s. a singleton $\{\mathcal{I}_\theta\}$.
  2. Fix $k \in \{1, \dots, d-1\}$. Let $N_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}^k$ denote the set of all $[\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_k]\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times k}$ such that
    $$\int_0^1\mathbb{P}\bigg(X_t\in\bigg[{\color{royalblue}\mathfrak{L}_{\theta}\cap\bigcap_{i=1}^k\mathfrak{L}_{-\theta_i}}\bigg]\cup\bigg[{\color{red}\mathfrak{L}_{-\theta}\cap\bigcap_{i=1}^k\mathfrak{L}_{\theta_i}}\bigg]\bigg)\frac{dt}{t}<\infty.$$
    If $[\theta_1, \dots, \theta_k] \notin N_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}^k$, then $X_{\eta_\theta\pm\cdot}-\mathcal{I}_\theta$ immediately enters $\bigcap_{i=1}^k\mathfrak{L}_{-\theta_i}$ a.s. (i.e., either the ${\color{red}\text{pre-}\eta_\theta}$ or the ${\color{royalblue}\text{post-}\eta_\theta}$ process immediately enters $\bigcap_{i=1}^k\mathfrak{L}_{-\theta_i}$). In particular, $\mathfrak{N}(\mathcal{I}_\theta) \cap \text{cone}(\{\theta_1, \dots, \theta_k\}) = \{0\}$ a.s.
  3. If $\dim(\mathrm{span}\, N_\theta^k) < kd$, then $\dim \mathfrak{N}(\mathcal{I}_\theta) \leqslant d - k$ a.s.

Resolving the Global Problem

Absence of Corners ($k=1$): How does the local theorem prove our first main result on corners? When $k=1$ (i.e., the case of corners), we may pick a countable dense collection of directions $\theta$ and note that, if a corner existed, its normal cone would have non-empty interior, thus intersecting the chosen countable dense set, which yields a contradiction.

The General Resolution via Grassmannians: To forbid higher-dimensional singularities globally, we must prohibit non-trivial intersections with higher-dimensional cones, requiring algebraic topological arguments over Grassmannians $\mathrm{Gr}(k,d)$. More precisely, such arguments show that controlling a.s. non-intersections of $\mathfrak{N}(\mathcal{I}_\theta)$ with appropriate cones, applied over a suitable countable set, implies the a.s. bound on the dimension $\mathrm{dim}\,\mathfrak{N}(\mathcal{I}_\theta)$.

What remains to prove: Thus, to establish our final goal (bounding the dimension $\mathrm{dim}\,\mathfrak{N}(\mathcal{I}_\theta)$ as well as our main theorem), it remains to establish the regularity of cones for the pre-$\eta_\theta$ and post-$\eta_\theta$ processes.

Why $d \geqslant 3$ is Fundamentally Harder

Warning: The absence of singularities globally cannot be deduced purely from ensuring every $\mathcal{I}_\theta$ is regular via countable deterministic directions.

The Geometric Obstruction: If we construct a convex body with at least one singular point that is not a corner, and apply an absolutely continuous random rotation, then with probability 1, no deterministic countable set of directions will ever expose the singular line.

The Probabilistic Hurdle in $\mathbb{R}^d$: Even though we fixed the local problem (i.e., we can now give definitive conclusions), the a.s. global smoothness problem remains. This is a specific hurdle for probabilists since a.s. events can only be intersected countably many times to remain a.s., while other fields (like convex analysis) typically don't worry about such issues. If a surface is fixed, global smoothness is equivalent to local smoothness of every point. Adding "a.s." breaks this down, because countably many events may not induce global regularity if $d \geqslant 3$.

Part 4

The Subsampling Approach

Core methodological trick used to bypass the intractability of conditioned processes. Subsampling the path at directional ladder times makes the geometric analysis mathematically viable.

The Intractability Problem

To determine if the process visits $\mathfrak{L}_{-\theta'}$, we must analyse the post-infimum process $X_{\eta_\theta + \cdot} - \mathcal{I}_\theta$. This behaves like the process conditioned to stay in a half-space $\mathfrak{L}_\theta$. While this can be analysed using Doob $h$-transforms (e.g., Ivanovs and Thøstesen, 2022), the resulting laws are highly intractable. Extracting explicit 0-1 criteria from them is extremely difficult.

A Miraculous Simplification

Instead of analysing the full conditioned path, we subsample the process at times when it is "close" to the orthogonal plane $\theta^\perp$ anchored at the exposed point. We trade full path information for analytical tractability, projecting coordinate by coordinate.

Geometric Separation

Under general conditions, a direction $\theta$ exposes a single point $\mathcal{I}_\theta$.

To check if another vector $\theta'$ exposes $\mathcal{I}_\theta$, we simply check if the path intersects the positive half-space $\mathfrak{L}_{-\theta'}$ anchored at $\mathcal{I}_\theta$.

If the path immediately enters the intersection of $k$ half spaces $\bigcap_{i=1}^k\mathfrak{L}_{-\theta_i}$, no positive combination $\sum_{i=1}^k \lambda_i\theta_i$ of the normal vectors can expose $\mathcal{I}_\theta$.

We forbid uncountably many directions from being in the normal cone using a single event.

This guarantees the actual post-infimum process $X_{\eta_\theta + \cdot} - \mathcal{I}_\theta$ immediately enters $C$ (since $\Gamma$ is immediately active and $\Gamma_{0+}=0$ by right-continuity), which is sufficient to rule out normal cone intersections.

Part 5

Fluctuation Machinery

The final step is to rigorously analyse the subsampled process. We build the machinery as follows: transforming the post-infimum into a running supremum, analysing the time-changed process via fluctuation theory, and characterising regular points via an extended Rogozin criterion.

Technically, we use $T=\infty$ and exponential killing. The actual intuition is that the results will hold a.s. simultaneously for a sequence of i.i.d. standard exponential times, so the strong law of large numbers and a density argument closes the gap, pushing everything back to deterministic times.

Logic Flowchart

Our goal is to prove that the time-changed process immediately enters a cone $C$ (in an appropriate class) almost surely:

$$X_{\eta_\theta + \Gamma^\theta} - \mathcal{I}_\theta \text{ immediately enters } C \text{ a.s.}$$

where $\Gamma^\theta$ is the right-continuous inverse local time of the reflected process of $\langle \theta,X\rangle$ at its running supremum.

This guarantees the actual post-infimum process $X_{\eta_\theta + \cdot} - \mathcal{I}_\theta$ enters $C$, which is necessary for ruling out normal cone intersections.

Bertoin's Excursion Reversal

Using time-reversal on excursions away from the directional extrema, we obtain a multidimensional extension of Bertoin's identity, which morally says that the analysis of the process post all-time infimum is equivalent to the analysis of the running supremum of the same process starting from 0.

Theorem (Chaumont, GC, Mijatović, 2026+).
Fix $\theta\in\mathbb{R}^d$ such that $I_{\theta}>-\infty$ a.s. and $\langle\theta,X\rangle$ immediately enters $(-\infty,0)$ a.s. Then, $$(( X - \underline{X}^\theta, \underline{X}^\theta - \mathcal{I}_\theta )_{\eta_\theta + t}); t\geqslant 0) \stackrel{d}{=} ( (R, \overline{X}^\theta)_t; t\geqslant 0 ).$$

Here, $\underline{X}^\theta$ and $\overline{X}^\theta$ track $X$ as $\langle \theta,X\rangle$ attains its future infimum or running supremum, respectively: consider the time of the ultimate infimum prior to $t$,

$$\underline{\tau}^\theta_t := \sup\left\{s \in [0,t] : \langle \theta, X_s \rangle \wedge \langle \theta, X_{s-} \rangle = \inf_{u\in[0,t]}\langle \theta, X_u \rangle\right\},$$

Then $\underline{X}^\theta$ is defined to record the position of $X$ at this time:

$$\underline{X}^\theta_t := \begin{cases} X_{\underline{\tau}^\theta_t}, & \langle \theta, X_{\underline{\tau}^\theta_t} \rangle \leqslant \langle \theta, X_{\underline{\tau}^\theta_t-} \rangle, \\ X_{\underline{\tau}^\theta_t-}, & \langle \theta, X_{\underline{\tau}^\theta_t} \rangle > \langle \theta, X_{\underline{\tau}^\theta_t-} \rangle. \end{cases}$$

The process $\overline{X}^\theta$ is defined similarly using the times at which $\langle \theta,X\rangle$ attains new suprema $\overline{\tau}^\theta_t$. The process $R$ is defined as a time and space reversal excursion-by-excursion of the reflected process above its running supremum:

$$R_t := (\overline{X}^{\theta}-X)_{(d_t+g_t-t)-}$$

where $g_t$ and $d_t$ are the left and right endpoints of the excursion interval straddling $t$.

No complex conditionings are required!

Multidimensional Fluctuation Identities

Pecherskii-Rogozin in $\mathbb{R}^d$ (Tamura & Tanaka, 2002).

If $\zeta\sim\mathrm{Exp}(\lambda)$ is an independent exponential time with rate $\lambda>0$, then the vectors $(\overline{\tau}^{\theta}_\zeta,\overline{X}^{\theta}_\zeta)$ and $(\zeta-\overline{\tau}^{\theta}_\zeta,X_\zeta-\overline{X}^{\theta}_\zeta)$ in $\mathbb{R}^{d+1}$ are independent and infinitely divisible and, for $(\phi,u)\in\mathbb{R}_+\times\mathbb{R}^{d}$, we have

$$\begin{aligned}\mathbb{E}\Big[e^{-\phi\overline{\tau}^{\theta}_\zeta + i\langle u,\overline{X}^\theta_\zeta\rangle}\Big] &= \exp\bigg(\!\int_0^\infty\!\!\int_{\mathrm{cl}\,\mathfrak{L}_{\theta}}\! (e^{-\phi t + i\langle u,x\rangle}-1) \mathbb{P}(X_t\in dx)\frac{dt}{t}\bigg)\\ &= \frac{\kappa_{\theta}(\lambda,0)}{\kappa_{\theta}(\lambda+\phi,u)},\\ \mathbb{E}\Big[e^{-\phi(\zeta-\overline{\tau}^{\theta}_\zeta) + i\langle u, X_\zeta-\overline{X}^{\theta}_\zeta\rangle}\Big] &= \exp\bigg(\!\int_0^\infty\!\!\int_{\mathfrak{L}_{-\theta}}\! (e^{-\phi t + i\langle u,x\rangle}-1)\mathbb{P}(X_t\in dx)\frac{dt}{t}\bigg),\end{aligned}$$

where we define

$$\quad\kappa_\theta(\phi, \vartheta) :=\exp\bigg(\int_0^\infty\!\!\int_{\mathrm{cl}\,\mathfrak{L}_\theta}\! (e^{-t} - e^{-\phi t + i\langle \vartheta, x\rangle}) \mathbb{P}(X_t \in dx) \frac{dt}{t}\bigg).$$

Fristedt in $\mathbb{R}^d$ (Chaumont, GC, Mijatović, 2026+).

The time-changed process $(id, X) \circ \Gamma^\theta_t$ is a killed Lévy process with exponent

$$\log \mathbb{E}[e^{\langle(-\phi, i\vartheta), Y_1\rangle}] = -c_0 \kappa_\theta(\phi, \vartheta) + q_0 - q_\lambda$$

Here, $q_0$ and $q_\lambda$ are just killing rates, where $\lambda$ is the killing rate of the original process $X$ and $\Gamma^\theta$ is the inverse local time of the reflected process of $\langle \theta,X\rangle$ at its running supremum.

The ABCD Identity in $\mathbb{R}^d$ (Theorem; Chaumont, GC, Mijatović, 2026+).

If $\langle\theta,X\rangle$ immediately enters $(0,\infty)$, then the following measures agree

$$\mathbb{P}(L^\theta_{\sigma^{\theta}(x^\theta)} \in ds, X_t \in dx) \frac{dt}{t} = \mathbb{P}(\Gamma^\theta_s \in dt, X \circ \Gamma^\theta_s \in dx) \frac{ds}{s}$$

for $(s,t,x)\in(0,\infty)\times(0,\infty)\times\mathfrak{L}_\theta$, where $\sigma^{\theta}(u) := \inf\{t>0 : \langle\theta,X_t\rangle > u\}$ is the first time $\langle\theta,X_t\rangle$ hits $(u,\infty)$, $u>0$.

This identity was first established in $\mathbb{R}$ by Alili & Chaumont by extending a result of Bertoin & Doney. We note that the process $L^\theta\circ \sigma^\theta$ is the right-continuous inverse of $X^\theta\circ \Gamma^\theta$.

Extending Rogozin's Criterion to Cones via Subsampling

Theorem (Chaumont, GC, Mijatović, 2026+). Fix $\theta_1, \dots, \theta_n \in \mathbb{R}^d_0$. If $$\int_0^1 \mathbb{P}\left(X_t \in \bigcap_{k=1}^n \mathfrak{L}_{\theta_k}\right) \frac{dt}{t} = \infty,$$ then $X$ immediately enters $\bigcap_{k=1}^n \mathfrak{L}_{\theta_k}$ a.s.

Proof. We proceed by induction on $n$.

Base Case $n=1$: Classical Rogozin's criterion: $\langle\theta_1,X\rangle$ is a 1D Lévy process that immediately becomes positive.

Inductive Step: Suppose true for $n-1$.

  1. By Rogozin, $Y := \langle\theta_1, X\rangle$ enters $(0, \infty)$ immediately because $\bigcap_{k=1}^n \mathfrak{L}_{\theta_k}\subset\mathfrak{L}_{\theta_1}$ and hence $$\int_0^1\mathbb{P}(\langle \theta_1,X_t\rangle>0)\frac{dt}{t}=\infty.$$
  2. Fristedt's theorem ensures $X \circ \Gamma^{\theta_1}$ is a Lévy process. Crucially, this Lévy process is immediately active (i.e., $\Gamma^{\theta_1}$ is a subordinator), which is important because immediate events are still immediate even after time-changing, which would not be the case if the regularity of Step 1 failed.
  3. The ABCD identity gives: $$\int_0^1 \mathbb{P}\left(X \circ \Gamma_t^{\theta_1} \in \bigcap_{k=2}^n \mathfrak{L}_{\theta_k}\right) \frac{dt}{t} = \infty$$
  4. By the inductive hypothesis, $X \circ \Gamma^{\theta_1}$ immediately enters $\bigcap_{k=2}^n \mathfrak{L}_{\theta_k}$ (and thus the full intersection).
  5. Since $\Gamma^{\theta_1}$ is a subordinator, the original process $X$ also enters immediately. $\blacksquare$
